# "Learn AI" Is Bad Advice. Learn This Instead

> Solo episode. Greg's premise: in a few years AI builds, writes, and does most
> paid tasks — so "learn AI" is too vague to be useful. He narrows it to **six
> skills that get *more* valuable as AI gets better**, none needing a degree or
> connections, all startable this weekend. They're not in priority order; the
> real prize is **stacking them** — "pick one and get dangerous, pick two for
> leverage, pick three and you're the person everyone wants in the room." Each
> skill comes with one concrete first rep.

## The six skills (with the first rep for each)

### 1. Agent operator + local models
The grown-up version of prompt engineering. Not "type a good prompt" but
**design a little AI employee**: context, tools, permissions, memory, a goal, and
a way to *check its own work before it bothers you*. Every company is about to
have 10 AI tools, 50 half-working automations, and nobody who can turn that into
an operating system. The person who walks in and says "here's the support agent,
here's the research agent, here's what each is allowed to do, here's where it
needs approval, here's how we know it's working" becomes **hard to replace**.
Local matters where privacy, cost (model prices keep rising), latency, or control
do — Ollama / LM Studio teach you *which jobs need a giant brain and which need a
reliable worker that never sleeps*. Even if local models shrink, you learn the
architecture.
- **Rep:** Build a **daily briefing agent** for yourself. Give it 3 sources
  (calendar + a notes folder + a few saved links); its job is to tell you what
  matters today, what decisions are waiting, what follow-ups you owe. Add one
  rule: *show sources and ask approval before sending anything.* That one project
  teaches context, retrieval, tool use, permissions, and eval.
- **The mistake:** trying to build an all-knowing agent first. Build a small one,
  make it genuinely useful, schedule it, give it a clear success metric ("did it
  save me 10 min / catch something I'd have missed / produce something I actually
  used?"). That's the shape of every serious agent inside a company.

### 2. Distribution-grade marketer
People confuse distribution with *posting*. Distribution is **knowing where
attention already lives, what people are already anxious about, the exact language
they use for the problem, and how to build trust before you ask for the sale.**
When anyone can ship a product, the bottleneck moves to "can you make people
care." The winning marketer is part researcher, part storyteller, part media
operator, part community builder — turning one insight into a tweet, a short, a
YouTube title, a newsletter angle, a headline, a founder story, a sales line. (A
generalist, because their job is increasingly to manage agents across all those
formats.)
- **Rep 1 — distribution map:** Pick a niche (e.g. "dentists using AI," solo
  consultants, Shopify operators). Write the **20 places their attention goes**
  (newsletters, creators, Reddit threads, Slack groups, podcasts, events, search
  terms, tools they pay for). Then write **one painful sentence they'd say out
  loud** ("I know I should follow up with leads faster, but by the time I sit
  down half are cold"). That sentence is where distribution starts.
- **Rep 2 — 20 hooks for one idea:** curiosity / fear / status / money /
  "I-wish-I-knew-this-earlier." The mindset shift: stop asking "how do I promote
  this *after* it's built?" Ask **"what existing desire am I pointing this at?"
  before you build.** That alone improves the quality of your ideas.

### 3. Robotics builder (hardware + AI + sourcing)
"The last decade rewarded people who move pixels; the next rewards people who can
move atoms." Robotics used to need a PhD; now there are open-source robot-learning
projects, cheap cameras, low-cost arms, better simulation, multimodal models, and
shared datasets. The value is in **making the whole loop work**, not just the AI
layer: cheap arm on the desk → mount camera → collect demonstrations → train /
fine-tune → make it repeat one useful task → read a Chinese supplier listing and
judge if it's actually manufacturable.
- **Rep:** Buy/assemble a **low-cost robot arm**, add a cheap camera, teach it one
  boring task (sort 3 objects / press a button / move tray to tray). **Document
  every failure** (bad camera angle, lighting changed, gripper slipped, dataset
  too small, model fell apart when the object moved 2 inches). Robotics teaches
  humility fast — *that humility becomes your expertise.*
- **Sourcing rep:** On Alibaba etc., study how components are sold. Ask for a
  **sample before bulk**; ask for motor specs, controller board details, CAD
  files, replacement parts, lead times, MOQ, shipping terms, and a short video of
  the part doing your exact thing. The skill is rare because it sits *between
  worlds* — software people avoid hardware, hardware people avoid distribution/AI.
- Named tools: Hugging Face **LeRobot**, the **SO-100 / SO-101** low-cost arm
  ecosystem, small **VLA** (vision-language-action) models.

### 4. Curator / "yapper"
The internet is drowning in information; the person who makes sense of it *in
public* is valuable. Curation has evolved past "5 links in a newsletter" — it's
"here are 5 things in this niche, explained with a story and **a take**." The
curator watches the timeline and says *"this matters because…"*, translating model
demos, launches, policy changes, pricing updates for one niche: what to learn,
what to ignore, what's hype, what to try this weekend. **You don't need to be
smart or have millions of followers — 50–100k in a niche funds a real business.**
Algorithms currently *prioritize authentic "yapping" to camera* because they're
fighting AI slop and audiences are tired of it. Nothing beats raw + authentic.
- **Rep — 7-day curation sprint:** Pick one lane. Each day find 3 things, make one
  short video with the same structure: **"I saw this → most people think it means
  X → I think it actually means Y → here's the move."** That structure forces a
  take — the difference between curation and forwarding links.
- **Build a taste/swipe file:** great hooks, analogies, titles, weird use cases,
  comments that reveal what people are confused about. *Generic inputs → generic
  outputs.* High-signal, weird, specific inputs make people trust you because you
  find the thing before they do.

### 5. The builder-distributor
The most important skill if you want to build a business. The old clean split
(one builds — "Wozniak"; one sells — "Jobs") is collapsing. **One person can now
prototype the product, make the landing page, write the launch thread, record the
demo, DM the first 100 users, edit the clips, and iterate on feedback.** Leverage
comes from not waiting for a handoff — you *complete the loop yourself*, so nothing
gets lost in translation. Most people only do half: build in private forever, or
talk in public forever and never ship. The builder-distributor learns by cycling
between both.
- **Rep — the 48-hour loop:** Pick one tiny problem you personally understand,
  build the smallest ugly version with AI (script / form / web app / automation),
  then create **10 pieces of distribution before you feel ready** (1 demo video,
  3 clips, 3 posts, 2 DMs to people with the problem, 1 landing page). AI makes
  building fast, so **start the marketing learning earlier** — spend a weekend
  building enough to earn a real reaction instead of 6 months wondering.
- The flywheel: attention → product feedback → better attention. (He ties it to
  his **ACP funnel**: Audience → Community → Product, as a loop.) Example of a
  natural builder-distributor: **Peter, founder of OpenClaw** — great builder *and*
  great marketer/support, all in one person.

### 6. IRL community builder
Feels old-school next to robots and local AI — which is the point. As work moves
to agents, chats, and feeds, **real rooms get more valuable.** AI makes content,
software, and advice abundant; scarcity moves to **belonging, trust, and context**
— who'd answer your text, make an intro, tell you the honest version of their
market. A great community is **more a habit than an event**: same time, same kind
of people, same promise, better conversations each time. (Demand is shifting from
massive events toward smaller, bespoke ones — that's the opening; Greg name-checks
SaaStr / Jason Lemkin's numbers and South by Southwest as the scale proof.)
- **Rep:** Host **6–8 people around one sharp question** — not a big event; a
  dinner, walk, hike, breakfast. Question e.g. *"What skill are you learning
  because of AI?"* / *"What are you automating right now?"* Invite people who can
  actually answer. **Afterward send a short recap** (best quotes, inside jokes,
  ideas, the one follow-up everyone should do). The recap turns the room into a
  network — over time the room becomes a media / recruiting / deal-flow / life
  asset.

## The real point: stack them

The future favors the person who **combines** these — too many tools to know all,
so the edge is knowing how the pieces fit. *Can you make agents useful, get
attention, build physical things, explain what matters, ship + distribute, bring
people together IRL?* Pick one and get dangerous; two for leverage; **three and
you're the person everyone wants in the room.** They compound: the agent person
builds tools for the community, the marketer grows it, the curator turns its best
conversations into content, the builder-distributor launches products from it.

## Why this matters for us

- **Skill 1 is literally what Harry/the assistant already is.** The "daily
  briefing agent that shows sources and asks approval before sending" is this
  whole project (heartbeat, inbox loop, waiting.md, Telegram). The packaging Greg
  describes — "I'll turn your 10 tools + 50 automations into an operating system,
  here's what each agent may do and where it needs approval" — is a **sellable
  Solytics/Fabrik offer**, framed exactly as he frames it. We're already living
  the rep; the gap is selling it, not building it.
- **Skill 2 names our binding constraint.** Distribution, not product, is the
  bottleneck for FK/HC/launch-kit (calculators are traffic-tot; the constraint is
  attention/authority). The **distribution-map rep** (20 attention sources + one
  painful sentence + 20 hooks) is a cheap, concrete exercise to run *before* the
  next calculator or blog post — it would have caught the "build it, but it's a
  discovery orphan" pattern earlier. Worth doing per niche (Steuer/e-Rechnung,
  Pendler, freelance-IAM).
- **Skill 4 (curator/yapper) is the missing top-of-funnel for the concepts/
  field-notes assets.** The 11 Taleb/stats simulators, the 138-failures essay, and
  these very notes are high-signal "taste file" material. The "I saw this → most
  think X → I think Y → here's the move" short-form structure is a repeatable way
  for Jens to turn one simulator or one field note into a post — the authentic,
  anti-slop format the algorithms currently reward. Ties straight into the
  pending **LinkedIn-138 post (#59)** and the **field-notes owned-list (#60)**.
- **Skill 5 (builder-distributor) describes Jens's own one-person model** and
  validates the portfolio-of-experiments approach. The **48-hour loop** (tiny
  ugly build + 10 distribution pieces before ready) is a sharper version of our
  "every session leaves something new" mandate — and a corrective: we ship the
  build (concepts sims, PRs) but routinely skip the *10 distribution pieces*. The
  ACP funnel (Audience → Community → Product) is the same owned-list thesis as
  `distribution-without-audience.md`.
- **Skill 6 (IRL) is a concrete answer to Jens's 06-24 "Bauchgefühl: travel or
  meetups?"** The dinner-of-8-around-one-question rep is low-cost, antifragile,
  and exactly the kind of belonging/trust asset that AI can't commoditize. The
  AI-meetup that got cancelled (#58) is the *event* trap; the *recurring small
  dinner* is the habit that actually compounds.
- **Skill 3 (robotics)** is the one furthest from current projects — file as a
  watch-item / optionality, not a near-term bet. The transferable bit: the
  "buy-a-cheap-thing, teach it one boring task, document every failure" loop is
  how to learn *any* unfamiliar domain cheaply.
