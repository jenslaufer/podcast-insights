# Howard Marks: AI Hurtles Ahead

> A faithful distillation of Howard Marks' Oaktree memo. AI has crossed from
> *productivity tool* to *labor replacement*, and it's adopting faster than any
> prior technology. The technology is real — Marks says so "with conviction" —
> but a real technology does not mean fairly-priced assets. The investor move is
> the same one he gave in *Is It a Bubble?*: a **moderate position, applied with
> selectivity and prudence**. Not all-in (risk of ruin), not all-out (risk of
> missing one of the great technological steps forward).

This Gist Talk episode is a third-party summary that narrates the memo closely;
it adds little independent analysis. The substance is the memo itself — read the
[standalone distillation](oaktree-ai-hurtles-ahead-memo.md). What follows is the
gist as the episode delivers it.

## Core message

AI is no longer a basic tool that answers questions — it is becoming an
**autonomous agent that executes independently**. You give it a goal and
parameters; it does the work, checks it, and hands back a finished product. That
is not assistance. That is replacement at the task level. And it arrived in
roughly two years, not the forty the computer took.

## The three capability levels

- **Level 1 — Chat AI.** Answers questions. Saves *thinking* time. (2023)
- **Level 2 — Tool-using AI.** Searches, analyzes, executes instructed tasks.
  Saves *execution* time, but bounded by your instructions. (2024)
- **Level 3 — Autonomous agents.** You give a goal; it produces the finished
  result. *"Labor replacement at the task level. Not assistance — replacement."*
  (now)

The Level 2 → 3 jump is the whole story: it *"separates a $50 billion market
from a multi-trillion-dollar one."*

## Training vs. inference (don't think "search engine")

- **Training** doesn't load information — it teaches the model *how to think*:
  reasoning patterns, argument structure, applying patterns to novel situations.
  Like a baby's brain developing through stimuli.
- **Inference** is everything it does afterward, in response to your **prompts**.
- Consequence: the model can't assign itself work, so output quality is capped by
  **prompt quality**. AI's potential is probably *underestimated* because users
  lack prompting skill — **the limit is the user, not the model.**

## Speed of adoption (the unprecedented part)

- The computer: ENIAC 1945 → IBM PC for general use only in the **early 1980s**,
  ~40 years. Ken Olsen (DEC), 1977: "no reason for any individual to have a
  computer in his home."
- AI: invisible (spam filters) before 2010 → generative AI framed as
  general-purpose **less than two years ago** → now **~400 million users and
  75–80% of companies**. Inference is **supply-constrained** — demand already
  exists rather than waiting for infrastructure to fill up.

## The investing takeaway

- AI already has many traits of a *good* investor: absorbs more data, remembers
  better, spots historical patterns, no fear/greed, no FOMO, no recency bias.
- Humans still win where AI is weakest: **novel situations** with no prior
  pattern; **qualitative judgment** (management quality, product, counterparties
  — the core of Oaktree's edge); and **skin in the game** — AI doesn't feel the
  weight of a concentrated position, so its risk-taking can be unconstrained.
- "Readily available, quantitative information about the present" can't deliver
  superior returns — everyone has it, and now AI processes it better than
  everyone. Edge moves to *judging the implications* and *divining the future*.
  Like indexation, **AI raises the bar and pushes out active managers who can't
  beat it.**

## The bubble verdict

The *technology* is not a fad — real, "with conviction." But that says nothing
about *prices*. Expect much capital to be **malinvested and destroyed** in the
infrastructure rush ("no reason to assume this time is different"). Hyperscalers
(profitable) are unlikely to be ruinously overpriced; unfunded startups with
multi-billion valuations and no product are **"lottery tickets."** Watch for
**circular revenue** (AI firms buying from each other) — the chain must rest on
end users paying for real value.

## The jobs concern (Marks "terribly concerned")

He can't see where enough new jobs come from, and the danger is **speed** —
displacement faster than society can retrain. Level 3 agents are "the automobile,
not a faster horse." Waymo already runs ~one-fifth of San Francisco taxi trips;
one ad shop reckons AI could replace 80% of its copy staff. Optimists note every
prior wave created new jobs; Marks concedes you can't prove a negative but is
"neither futurist enough nor optimist enough" to assume the jobs appear. Closes
on a friend's line: "would rather be an optimist and wrong than a pessimist and
right. Me too."

## One-line throughline

Believe the technology *and* discipline the price: moderate, selective, prudent —
because "real and transformative" and "fairly valued" are two different
questions.
