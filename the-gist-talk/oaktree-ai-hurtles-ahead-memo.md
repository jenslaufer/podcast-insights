# AI Hurtles Ahead (the memo)

> An addendum to *Is It a Bubble?*, written because the ground shifted in just
> ~12 weeks. Marks asks **Claude (Anthropic)** to teach him what changed in AI;
> the experience convinces him AI is real, autonomous, and adopting faster than
> anything before it. His conclusion is unchanged from December: a real,
> transformative technology is not the same as a fairly-priced asset. Take a
> **moderate, selective, prudent** position — neither all-in (risk of ruin) nor
> all-out (risk of missing a great technological step).

This is the primary resource. The [Gist Talk episode](howard-marks-ai-hurtles-ahead.md)
is a faithful summary of it.

## Origin story — Marks lets Claude teach him

A techie advisor suggested Marks ask Claude to build him a tutorial on AI. Claude
produced a personalized **nine-module curriculum** and a **~10,000-word essay**
built around Marks' *own* frameworks (the "sea change in interest rates," the
"pendulum of investor psychology"). Marks describes "awe" — it read like a note
from a brilliant colleague: argued logically, anticipated his rebuttals, used
humor, candidly flagged AI's own limits. Most quotations in the memo are Claude's
words.

## "Can AI think?" — the Buffett/Munger exchange (the memo's set piece)

The skeptic's framing: AI is "an extraordinarily impressive pattern matcher… a
very talented cover band, not a composer." Claude's rejoinder, aimed at Marks
personally:

> "Howard, everything you know about investing came from other people. Benjamin
> Graham taught you about margin of safety. Buffett taught you about quality.
> Charlie Munger taught you about mental models… The raw material came from
> others. **The synthesis was yours.** The question isn't where the inputs came
> from — it's whether the system, human or artificial, can combine them in ways
> that are genuinely novel and useful."

Marks concedes this is "completely true." Claude's clinching *economic* point:
even if it's "merely" pattern matching, if it does the work of a **$200,000-a-year
research associate**, the payer doesn't care whether it "really" thinks. The
operative question is *"does AI do the work?"* — a distinction without a
difference.

## The three capability levels (central framework)

- **Level 1 — Chat AI** — answers questions; saves *thinking* time. (2023)
- **Level 2 — Tool-using AI** — searches, analyzes, executes instructed tasks;
  saves *execution* time, but bounded. (2024)
- **Level 3 — Autonomous agents** — given a goal and parameters, does the work,
  checks it, delivers a finished product. *"Labor replacement at the task level.
  Not assistance — replacement."* (now)

The Level 2 → 3 leap *"separates a $50 billion market from a multi-trillion-dollar
one."*

## Training vs. inference

- **Training** teaches the model *how to think* (reasoning, argument structure,
  applying patterns to the unfamiliar) — not loading facts. Like a developing
  brain.
- **Inference** is the rest of its working life, driven by **prompts**.
- The model can't set its own tasks → output is capped by prompt quality. So
  AI's potential is likely **underestimated** because users can't prompt well —
  **the limitation is the user.**

## Speed — AI vs. the computer

- ENIAC 1945; Watson Sr.'s apocryphal "world market for maybe five computers";
  Ken Olsen (DEC) 1977: "no reason for any individual to have a computer in his
  home"; general-use IBM PC only in the **early 1980s — ~40 years after ENIAC.**
- AI: invisible (spam filters, recommendation engines) pre-2010 → Siri/Alexa →
  generative AI as a general-purpose technology **<2 years ago** → **~400M users,
  75–80% of companies.** Inference is **supply-constrained** — demand is already
  there.

## Capability timeline (via Matt Shumer, "Something Big Is Happening")

- 2022: couldn't reliably do arithmetic (7×8 = 54, confidently).
- 2023: passed the bar exam.
- 2024: wrote working software, explained graduate-level science.
- Late 2025: top engineers handed most coding to AI.
- **2026-02-05:** OpenAI **GPT-5.3 Codex** and Anthropic **Opus 4.6** ship the
  same day; Shumer describes an outcome, walks away four hours, returns to
  finished work. OpenAI documents GPT-5.3 Codex as "our first model that was
  instrumental in creating itself." Dario Amodei: AI writes "much of the code" at
  Anthropic; possibly **1–2 years** from AI autonomously building the next
  generation.
- Marks' distinction: railroads/computers/internet were *labor-saving*; AI is
  different *in kind* — it has **autonomy** and may do tasks "that didn't exist
  before AI dreamed them up."

## Limitations (Claude's own admissions)

- Unresolved whether AI can solve **truly unprecedented** problems with no
  training-data pattern — weakest exactly where human judgment matters most.
- **Hallucinations** — not always aware it doesn't know; not error-free.
- Bounded **context window** / working memory.
- Its fluency can lend it *excessive credibility* ("Claude can make mistakes.
  Please double-check responses.").
- Raises the **HAL 9000** question of autonomy and loss of control.

## Implications for investing

- **Tell:** software stocks fell ~7% around 2026-02-03 despite Anthropic's coding
  business growing for a year — humans fail to price in new information
  (cognitive dissonance, anchoring).
- AI resembles a good investor (more data, better memory, pattern recognition, no
  fear/greed/FOMO/recency bias).
- Humans still beat it on: **novel situations** (no prior pattern), **qualitative
  judgment** (management, product, counterparties), and **skin in the game** —
  AI doesn't feel capital loss, so its risk-taking may be unconstrained.
- Echoing Andrew Marks (*Something of Value*, 2021): quantitative info about the
  present can't yield superior returns — now AI processes it better than anyone.
  Edge must come from judging *implications*, qualitative assessment, and
  divining companies' futures. **Like indexation, AI raises the bar and pushes
  out active managers who can't beat it.**
- AI proposes **"hypotheses"** about the future from past patterns; investors must
  still **check them for reasonableness** before acting — a place superior humans
  add value.

## The bubble verdict — five questions

1. **Tech a fad/illusion?** No — "with conviction," very real.
2. **Application a distant dream?** No — already in large-scale demand; potential
   more likely underestimated than exaggerated.
3. **Are infrastructure builders unwise?** Like every sweeping innovation, the
   rush will see much capital **"malinvested" and destroyed** — "no reason to
   assume this time will be different."
4. **Will infrastructure earn adequate returns?** Unknowable — "We'll know in 10
   years."
5. **Are valuations irrational?** Hyperscalers (Microsoft, Amazon, Google) —
   hugely profitable — unlikely to prove "ruinously excessive"; OpenAI/Anthropic
   IPO valuations unknown until listed; **unfunded startups with multi-billion
   valuations and no product are "lottery tickets"** — most worthless, a few make
   winners rich.

**Inference capex now exceeds training capex** — and inference responds to *actual
demand* generating real revenue, which validates it. But the pipeline build could
still outrun demand, and some AI revenue is **"circular"** (AI firms buying from
each other) — the chain must ultimately rest on end users paying for real value.
Claude only argued the *technology* is real, not that *prices* are appropriate.

## The actionable conclusion (carried verbatim from *Is It a Bubble?*)

> "No one should go all-in without acknowledging that they face the risk of ruin
> if things go badly. But… no one should stay all-out and risk missing out on one
> of the great technological steps forward. A **moderate position, applied with
> selectivity and prudence**, seems like the best approach."

## Postscript — jobs (Marks "terribly concerned")

Can't see where enough new jobs come from; the danger is **speed**. Level 3
agents are "the automobile, not a faster horse." Across legal associates,
analysts, consultants, engineers, compliance officers, claims adjusters — a labor
market in the **trillions/year**; even handling 30–50% of structured work means
**$150–250B in annual labor value migrating to AI compute**. Waymo already runs
~one-fifth of San Francisco taxi trips. Optimists cite history (every wave created
new jobs); Marks grants the case isn't unreasonable but is "neither futurist
enough nor optimist enough" to trust it. Closes: a friend "would rather be an
optimist and wrong than a pessimist and right. Me too."

## Context — the predecessor memo

*Is It a Bubble?* (2025-12-09) declined the "bubble" label because the key
ingredient — **psychological excess / mania** — wasn't yet present. On valuation:
P/Es in the **Nifty-Fifty bubble (1969–72)** were even higher than **1998–2000**;
**dot-com** P/Es for Microsoft, Cisco, Oracle ran *much higher* than today's AI
leaders (Nvidia, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta). I.e. current AI valuations
are *less extreme on a P/E basis* than prior manias. *AI Hurtles Ahead* updates
this toward "AI's potential is underestimated — but that doesn't make the assets
fairly priced."
