# Overcoming Negativity Bias Through Tiny Experiments

> Why does *one* rejection override dozens of wins? Le Cunff traces it to
> **negativity bias** — the brain weights threats heavier than rewards because,
> ancestrally, rejection meant expulsion from the group, which meant death. You
> can't reason your way out of it (rational thought doesn't override fear). The
> escape is behavioural: stop setting rigid goals, run **tiny experiments**
> instead — small, time-bound actions you commit to *without judging the outcome*,
> then review honestly. Action rewires the brain where argument can't.

## The negativity bias (the why behind the fear)
- **Negative stimuli trigger stronger brain responses** than positive or neutral
  ones. It shows up early — *even three-month-olds* display it in social
  interaction. It's wiring, not weakness.
- **Evolutionary logic:** ancestors who fixated on threats survived and passed on
  genes. Useful on the savanna, miscalibrated for modern life where "raising your
  hand" is not a death risk.
- **Downstream effects:** risk aversion, loss aversion (avoiding a loss beats an
  equal gain), and *negative news reads as more true* than positive news. The bias
  quietly vetoes anything new.
- **The self-consistency fallacy:** "I've always been this way, so I must stay this
  way." Past rejection hardens into a limiting identity narrative that shrinks the
  space of what feels possible.

## Why willpower fails — and what works
- **Rational thinking cannot override fear.** Telling yourself "this is irrational"
  doesn't dissolve the bias.
- **Exposure rewires the brain.** Like exposure therapy: small, repeated, survivable
  doses of the feared thing recalibrate the threat response. You act your way into
  a new self-image, not think your way.
- **Metacognition is the lever:** notice the bias *while* it's running ("I'm
  ruminating on one bad event"), name it, and choose the next small action anyway.

## The Tiny Experiments framework
Replace **linear goals** ("I will achieve X by date Y") with a **circular model of
growth**: goals are discovered, pursued, and adapted in conversation with the world.

- **PACT** — a good experiment is **P**urposeful, **A**ctionable, **C**ontinuous,
  **T**rackable.
- **The format:** *"I will [action] for [duration]."*
  - e.g. "I will write a weekly newsletter for six weeks."
  - e.g. "I will meditate every morning for one month."
- **Two essentials only:** what you're testing + the number of trials. No elaborate
  prep — act with the tools and circumstances you already have.
- **Right-size the duration:** *long enough to collect real data, short enough that
  you'll actually do it.*
- **Withhold judgment during the run.** Unexpected results are data, not failure —
  this is what defuses the negativity bias. There is no "fail," only signal.

## After the trial — the steering decision
Review the data, then choose one of three (no permanent verdicts):
- **Persist** — it's working, continue unchanged.
- **Pause** — shelve it temporarily, no abandonment.
- **Pivot** — adjust a parameter and run it again.

## Her own arc (the credibility behind it)
- Left **Google** after a health scare → launched a startup *for the wrong reasons*
  (status, others' expectations) → it failed → the failure was **liberating**, not
  catastrophic. Founded **Ness Labs** from curiosity instead.
- Lesson: ambition built on *what impresses others* is fragile; ambition built on
  *what makes a life worth living* compounds.

## Mindful vs. toxic productivity
- **Toxic productivity:** output as self-worth, driven by fear and others' approval
  → the road to burnout.
- **Mindful productivity:** **intentional imperfection** — pick where "good enough"
  is the right target, protect attention, and let curiosity (not anxiety) set the
  pace. Procrastination is treated as a *compass* (a signal about misalignment),
  not a moral failing.

## Tiny practices to steal
- **Awareness:** when ruminating on one bad event, label it "negativity bias" out loud.
- **Self-reflection through writing:** journal as a thinking tool to see thoughts objectively.
- **Positive routines:** redirect attention to an uplifting action when the spiral starts.
- **Learn in public** to dissolve fear of judgment — a tiny experiment in itself.
- **One-line maxim:** *"What we focus on, we empower and enlarge."*
